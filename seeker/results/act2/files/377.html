According to Shokhin, the current parliament is likely to serve out its term, and Communists will shun confrontation so as to preserve their Duma apparatus as a base for future electoral efforts. He also said he expects deputies from the Agrarian Party to become more cooperative with the government, particularly when the Duma resumes discussion of the land code, which was recently rejected by the Federation Council. -- Laura Belin in MoscowADVISERS OFFER DIFFERENT ASSESSMENTS OF MEDIA'S ROLE. When asked about the role of the media in the campaign, presidential advisers not surprisingly praised the contribution made by pro-Yeltsin journalists. Mark Urnov, who directed Yeltsin's analytical center before leaving to work full time within campaign structures, suggested that the Russian mass media had improved their professionalism during the campaign. He argued that before January 1996, Russia had only an opposition press -- either left-wing (pro-communist) or liberal. Since January 1996, Urnov claimed, the media had taken a great step forward toward a "normal, democratic and stable" institution by both criticizing and supporting the president. (In fact, criticism of Yeltsin in the liberal media was almost entirely absent during the months before the election.) Andranik Migranyan disagreed with Urnov; he argued that it was "naive" to believe that the current relationship between the media and authorities is normal. Georgii Satarov admitted that the media had not been objective during the campaign, but said it was unfair for critics to compare Russia to developed western democracies in which elections do not threaten the entire political system. He noted that democratic procedures had allowed figures like Hitler to come to power. Given the fact that these elections threatened to reverse Russia's democratic progress, Satarov argued, it was not merely justifiable but necessary for the media to work for the president in the short term in order to protect their independence in the long term. When asked by one journalist whether the media should primarily report the news rather than do propaganda work for one candidate, Satarov replied angrily that Russia's three main television networks themselves decided that in order to preserve their ability to report the news in the future, they had to do "propaganda work" this spring. -- Laura Belin in Moscow This material was reprinted with permission of the Open Media Research Institute, a nonprofit organization with research offices in Prague, Czech Republic.
